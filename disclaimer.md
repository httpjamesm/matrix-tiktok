### Notice to ByteDance, TikTok, and Any Party Considering a Takedown Request

Before submitting any takedown notice, cease-and-desist, or other legal demand targeting
this repository, the sending party is strongly advised to review the following. Submitting
a notice without doing so may expose the sender to **adverse cost awards, statutory
liability, and reputational consequences**.

---

#### 1. This Project Is Explicitly Protected Under Canadian Law

This project is developed in Canada and is governed by Canadian copyright law. The
**primary legal protection** for this work is **Section 41.12 of the *Copyright Act*
(R.S.C., 1985, c. C-42)**, as amended by *S.C. 2024, c. 27*.

Section 41.12(1) provides that the prohibition on circumventing technological protection
measures **"does not apply to a person who circumvents a technological protection measure
that protects a lawfully obtained computer program for the purpose of"**:

> **(a)** *"obtaining information that would allow the person to make the program or a
> device in which it is embedded interoperable with any other computer program, device
> or component"*; or
>
> **(b)** *"making the program or a device in which it is embedded interoperable with
> any other computer program, device or component."*

Section 41.12(4) further permits the author to **"communicate the information obtained
under that paragraph to another person for the purposes of allowing that person to make
the computer program or a device in which it is embedded interoperable with any other
computer program, device or component."**

The TikTok application used in the development of this project was lawfully obtained
through official public channels. No TikTok source code is reproduced in this project.
This project falls squarely within the scope of s. 41.12.

#### 2. Canadian Courts Have Full Discretion to Award Costs Against Bad Faith Claimants

**Section 34(3)** of the *Copyright Act* provides:

> *"The costs of all parties in any proceedings in respect of the infringement of a right
> conferred by this Act shall be in the discretion of the court."*

Canadian courts routinely exercise this discretion to award costs — including substantial
indemnity costs — against parties who bring or threaten proceedings in bad faith or
without reasonable grounds. A copyright claim brought against this project, with knowledge
of the s. 41.12 interoperability exception, would be a strong candidate for an adverse
costs award.

Additionally, **Section 38.1(5)** of the *Copyright Act* requires courts to consider
**"the good faith or bad faith"** of the parties when assessing any award of statutory
damages. Bad faith conduct by a claimant is a relevant factor that courts may weigh
against them.

#### 3. Canada's Notice-and-Notice Regime Prohibits Abusive Notices

Canada does **not** operate a "notice-and-takedown" regime. Under **Sections 41.25–41.26**
of the *Copyright Act*, copyright holders may send a "notice of claimed infringement" to
an internet intermediary, which must forward it to the subscriber — but **no takedown
is required or automatic**.

Critically, **Section 41.25(3)** explicitly prohibits a notice of claimed infringement
from containing:

> *"(a) an offer to settle the claimed infringement; (b) a request or demand, made in
> relation to the claimed infringement, for payment or for personal information; [or]
> (c) a reference, including by way of hyperlink, to such an offer, request or demand."*

As the Government of Canada has confirmed: **"U.S. copyright fines and penalties do not
apply in Canada."** Any notice that attempts to leverage U.S. statutory damages or
settlement demands against a Canadian subscriber is non-compliant with Canadian law.

#### 4. GitHub's DMCA Process — A Note on Jurisdiction

GitHub, Inc. is a U.S.-based platform and processes takedown notices under the U.S.
**Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA), 17 U.S.C. § 512**. Any notice submitted to
GitHub must be made **under penalty of perjury** and must reflect a **good faith belief**
that the material is infringing.

GitHub's own policy states that if a copyright owner does not file a valid legal complaint
in a court of competent jurisdiction within **10–14 days** of a counter-notice being
submitted, GitHub will **re-enable the disabled content**.

A party that submits a DMCA notice to GitHub against this repository — knowing that the
underlying activity is protected by the Canadian *Copyright Act* s. 41.12 interoperability
exception — does so without a good faith basis. Such a notice would be submitted under
penalty of perjury with knowledge of a complete legal defence.
